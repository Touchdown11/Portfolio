Components of Emotions

Understanding the three distinctive components of emotional experiences

Subjective Experience

The feeling component of emotions that is created in our brains. Emotions make us act in certain ways and are essential for a meaningful life.

  • Aids our survival
  • Socially important
  • Creates motivation to act

Physiological Response

The bodily changes that occur with emotions, such as increased heart rate, sweating, or changes in facial expressions.

  • Darwin's theory of adaptive expressions
  • Mirror neurons for empathy
  • Nonverbal communication

Behavioral Response

How we express emotions through actions, postures, gestures, vocalizations, and facial expressions.

  • Communicates feelings to others
  • Influences social interactions
  • Helps interpret situations

Types of Emotions

Happiness/Joy

Mouth forming smile, cheeks lifted, twinkle in eye

Sadness

Brow's inner corner raised, mouth corners drawn down

Surprise

Brows raised, eyes widened, mouth rounded in oval shape

Fear

Brows level, drawn in and up, eyelids lifted, mouth corners retracted

Disgust

Nose wrinkled, upper lip raised, tongue pushed outward

Practical EI Exercises

Simple exercises to develop your emotional intelligence skills

Daily Emotional Check-in

Take 5 minutes each day to identify and name your emotions. Journaling can help track patterns over time.

The Pause Practice

When feeling strong emotions, consciously pause before reacting. Count to 10 and breathe deeply.

Perspective Taking

In conflicts, intentionally try to see the situation from the other person's viewpoint.

Active Listening

Practice listening without interrupting, then paraphrase what you heard to ensure understanding.

Gratitude Practice

Each day, identify three things you're grateful for to cultivate positive emotions.

Emotion Regulation

When upset, use techniques like deep breathing, visualization, or physical activity to regulate emotions.
